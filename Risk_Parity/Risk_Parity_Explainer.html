1 · What is Risk Parity?

Traditional portfolios like 60/40 allocate dollars roughly equally — but because equities are far more volatile than bonds, almost all of the risk ends up sitting in the equity sleeve. The portfolio is dollar-diversified but not risk-diversified.

60/40 Portfolio

60% equities, 40% bonds by dollar weight.

~90% of portfolio risk comes from equities.

Bonds contribute almost nothing to risk — capital is "wasted."

Risk Parity Portfolio

Weights chosen so every asset contributes equally to total risk.

Each asset: exactly 1/N of portfolio risk.

Low-vol assets (bonds, TIPS) get larger dollar weights to compensate.

Core belief: Over long horizons, all major risk assets deliver similar risk-adjusted returns. If true, the most diversified portfolio is the best long-run bet. Risk Parity is a diversification-first philosophy.

4 · Key Building Block: Total Risk Contribution (TRC)

Every covariance-aware Risk Parity method is built on TRC. Understanding it is the key to reading every output table.

Portfolio Volatility

σ_P = sqrt( w' Σ w )

Total Risk Contribution

TRC_i = w_i · (Σw)_i / σ_P Sum of all TRC_i = σ_P ← always true (Euler's homogeneous function theorem)

Percentage Risk Contribution (PRC)

PRC_i = TRC_i / σ_P = w_i · (Σw)_i / σ_P² ← sums to 1 (100%)

Equal Weight: PRC is NOT equal

With 20% in each asset, Asset 2 (equity) and Asset 3 (commodities) each contribute 40–50%+ of risk. Asset 1 and 5 (bonds) together contribute less than 10%. Dollar balance ≠ risk balance.

ERC goal: PRC = 20% each

The optimizer finds weights where every PRC_i = 1/N. To get there, Asset 1 and Asset 5 (bonds, TIPS) will receive much larger dollar weights than 20%, while Asset 2 and Asset 3 (equity, commodities) will shrink.

5 · The Seven Portfolios

Equal Weight  Current Portfolio / Benchmark

w_i = 1/N = 20% for all i

What it does: No optimization. Fixed forever at 20% per asset.

Asset lens: 20% Bonds + 20% Equity + 20% Commodities + 20% Hedge Fund + 20% TIPS. A balanced dollar mix, but equity and commodities dominate risk by a wide margin.

Client risk problem: Though it looks balanced, the 40% allocated to Asset 2 + Asset 3 likely accounts for 90%+ of portfolio risk. During the 2008 GFC, these two assets sold off severely — check the drawdown chart to see if EW breached the −15% client limit.

Vol RP  Naïve 1/σ

w_i = (1/σ_i) / Σ_j(1/σ_j) σ_i = sqrt(Σ_ii)

Asset lens: Asset 3 (commodities, σ = 5.93%) is ~8× more volatile than Asset 1 (bonds, σ = 0.72%). Vol RP will therefore give Asset 1 roughly eight times the weight of Asset 3. Bonds and TIPS dominate the allocation by dollar weight. No correlation information used — if bonds and TIPS happen to crash together, this offers no protection beyond the volatility signal.

Expected shape of rolling weights chart: The two bond-like assets (1 & 5) together should hold 60–70%+ of the portfolio. Asset 2 (equity) and Asset 3 (commodities) should be tiny, single-digit weights.

Covar RP (ERC)  Equal Risk Contribution

Minimize: Σ_i (TRC_i − σ_P/N)² subject to: Σ w_i = 1, w_i ≥ 0

Asset lens: Unlike Vol RP, ERC accounts for the fact that Asset 1 (bonds) and Asset 5 (TIPS) are highly correlated (r ≈ 0.79 from the heatmap above). Two correlated assets together contribute more risk than their individual volatilities suggest. ERC will therefore not give Asset 1 and Asset 5 quite as large a combined weight as Vol RP does — it penalizes their correlation and balances the risk more carefully across all five assets.

Check the TRC heatmap: If ERC worked, every cell in the Covar RP row of the heatmap should be close to 20%.

MDP  Maximum Diversification Portfolio

Maximize: DR_P = (Σ_i w_i σ_i) / σ_P subject to: Σ w_i = 1, w_i ≥ 0

Asset lens: MDP rewards assets that are volatile on their own but weakly correlated with everyone else — assets that provide "free" diversification. Asset 4 (hedge fund) has the lowest correlations with the other four assets (r ≈ 0.3–0.5). MDP may give Asset 4 a larger-than-expected weight because it "earns its keep" through diversification, even though its volatility is modest. The Diversification Ratio (DR) in the final snapshot table shows how much correlation reduction the portfolio achieves — MDP will always have the highest DR by construction.

GFC risk: In 2008, correlations across all risk assets spiked. The correlation structure MDP was optimized for broke down. Check the drawdown chart — MDP often underperforms in crises precisely because it relied on correlations remaining stable.

HRP  Hierarchical Risk Parity

  1. Distance matrix: d_ij = sqrt(0.5 · (1 − ρ_ij)) — correlated assets are "close."
  2. Cluster: Build a dendrogram (tree) by merging the two closest pairs repeatedly.
  3. Bisect: Allocate capital recursively, splitting proportional to inverse cluster variance.

Asset lens: The clustering step will discover two obvious groups from the correlation matrix: a rates cluster (Asset 1 + Asset 5, both bond-like, r ≈ 0.79) and a risk-on cluster (Asset 2 + Asset 3, both positively correlated and risk-sensitive). Asset 4 (hedge fund, lowest correlations) will form its own cluster or attach last. Within each cluster, assets compete for weight. Then the clusters compete against each other. This means Asset 1 and Asset 5 share a risk budget — they don't each independently draw from the full portfolio risk budget as they might in Vol RP or ERC.

Key advantage: No matrix inversion needed. Robust to highly correlated assets. When correlations spike in a crisis, the cluster structure adapts more gracefully than a direct optimizer.

Factor ERC  Kalman Factor-Implied Covariance

Σ_implied = B · Σ_F · B' + Σ_ε B[i,k] = Kalman-smoothed beta of asset i on factor k (time-varying) Σ_F = rolling 36m covariance of factor returns Σ_ε = diag(idiosyncratic variances)

Why this matters for our specific assets: The five assets share some common factors — particularly High Yield (all 5 assets include it as a cross-risk factor) and MSCI EM Equity (Assets 3, 4, 5). A standard covariance matrix from raw returns mixes all these overlaps together and is estimated with only 36 data points. The factor model decomposes it cleanly: "how much of Asset 3's risk comes from the commodities factor vs. EM equity vs. the credit spread?" Then it equalizes factor-level risk.

The Kalman filter makes betas time-varying. During the 2008 GFC, Asset 4 (hedge fund) likely increased its equity-like beta as forced deleveraging made all risk assets move together. A static OLS beta would miss this — Kalman catches it, meaning Factor ERC will have naturally reduced exposure to that asset during the stress period.

60/40  Market Benchmark

r_60/40,t = 0.60 × r_MSCI_World,t + 0.40 × r_Global_AGG,t

What it is: The classic institutional portfolio — 60% global equity (MSCI World index return), 40% global bonds (Global AGG index return). This is what the Risk Parity literature is explicitly trying to improve upon. Class 5-3 Slide 15 specifies it as the benchmark for comparison.

Important distinction: The 60/40 here uses the actual MSCI World and Global AGG index returns from the factors sheet — not any of the five client assets. It's a pure market benchmark. In the charts it appears as a dotted line.

6 · How the Rolling Window Works

  • Month t = 36 (Dec 2006): Collect the 36 monthly returns from Jan 2004 to Dec 2006. Compute the 5×5 covariance matrix (population, ddof=0). Run each RP optimizer. Record weights w(36).
  • Apply forward: Weights from month t are applied to month t+1 returns. This one-month lag is intentional — you can't rebalance before you've seen the data. December 2006 weights → January 2007 return.
  • Roll forward: At t = 37, use Feb 2004 – Jan 2007. New covariance, new weights. Repeat through t = 120 (Dec 2013).
  • Result: 84 monthly portfolio returns (Jan 2007 – Dec 2013). The first 36 months are "burned" for initialization — this is standard practice.

Factor-Level % Risk Contribution rp-ftrc

Breaks portfolio risk into contributions from the 12 union factors (from the Kalman model) plus idiosyncratic (unexplained) variance.

Portfolio factor exposure: b_P,k = Σ_i w_i · β_i,k Factor TRC: FTRC_k = b_P,k · (Σ_F b_P)_k / σ_P_sys Idiosyncratic share = Σ_i w_i² σ²_ε,i / σ²_P
Factor-level % risk contribution by portfolio method. "Idiosyncratic" (rightmost segment) is the share not explained by any factor. The key question: does Factor ERC show the most balanced distribution across factors? Watch for High Yield (credit risk) — it appears in all five asset models as a cross-risk factor, so it may dominate risk across all methods regardless of how the weights are set. If so, the portfolio has a latent credit risk concentration regardless of which RP variant you pick.

Which Method to Recommend?

  • Covar RP (ERC) — academic standard, guaranteed equal PRC, easiest to explain
  • HRP — most robust, no matrix inversion, handles the bond/TIPS correlation gracefully
  • Factor ERC — tells the most complete story across pages 2, 3 & 5; connects the Kalman analysis to the portfolio proposal
The key narrative for the client: Under equal weighting, Asset 2 (US equity) and Asset 3 (commodities) are consuming 90%+ of the risk budget despite representing only 40% of the dollars. Both sold off sharply in 2008. Risk Parity rebalances the risk budget — the two bond-like assets (Asset 1 and Asset 5) are underweighted in dollar terms relative to what their risk contribution demands. Increasing bond/TIPS weight and reducing equity/commodities weight is the mechanism by which all RP methods improve the drawdown profile.
