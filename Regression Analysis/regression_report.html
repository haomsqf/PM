Executive Summary

Five unknown assets are analyzed using a two-stage factor regression framework. All asset returns are expressed as excess returns (asset minus a risk-free rate proxy) so that regression intercepts estimate Jensen's Alpha and factor loadings reflect pure risk premia. The analysis progresses from simple single-factor screening through a full multivariate model, multicollinearity diagnostics, systematic feature engineering, and finally a combined regression incorporating both the original factor universe and all constructed spread features.

Methodology

💡
Why excess returns? Subtracting the risk-free rate (proxied by the Cash factor) from each asset return shifts the regression intercept from a raw average return to Jensen's Alpha — the return earned above what the factor exposures alone would predict. This mirrors the CAPM framework and ensures factor betas measure pure risk premia rather than blends of risk premium and rate of return.
1

Single-Factor Screening — 135 regressions

Each of the 5 assets is regressed separately on each of the 27 factors (plus HY‑GAGG), producing one slope (beta), p-value, and R² per pair. This establishes which factors carry statistically meaningful signal for each asset in isolation. Threshold: p ≤ 0.05.

2

Full 28-Factor OLS — joint model per asset

All 28 factors enter simultaneously for each asset. Factors that were significant in isolation often lose significance here due to multicollinearity. The contrast between single-factor and full-model p-values motivates the feature engineering step.

3

VIF Diagnostics — two passes per asset

Variance Inflation Factors are computed on (a) the single-factor-significant subset and (b) the full-model-significant subset for each asset separately, flagging pairwise multicollinearity at VIF > 5 (warning) and VIF > 10 (severe). Perfectly collinear pairs (VIF = ∞) are noted.

4

Feature Engineering — systematic spread construction

For every factor pair with |correlation| > 0.70 where both factors are significant in at least one single-factor model and at least one degrades in the joint model, a spread feature is constructed: A − B for positively correlated pairs (isolates what A captures beyond B), A + B for negatively correlated pairs (combines opposing directional signals). All qualifying pairs in this dataset were positively correlated, yielding 120 difference features.

5

Combined Model — 28 originals + 120 spreads, per asset

All 148 features enter jointly. Significant factors are color-coded: green for original factors, blue for constructed spreads. VIF and heatmaps are re-run on the full combined set to assess whether spread features add explanatory power over and above the original factor universe.

PHASE 3

VIF Analysis

Variance Inflation Factors quantify how much multicollinearity inflates each coefficient's standard error. VIF is computed in two passes per asset: first on the subset of single-factor-significant factors, then on the full-model-significant subset. Both passes use the same thresholds.

Key findings from the VIF pass: the hedge fund strategy indices (Long Short HF, Event Driven HF, HF FOF Div, Relative Value HF) are highly intercorrelated (pairwise correlations 0.85–0.93), producing severe or perfect VIF when included together. Asset 4's significant factors include multiple HF indices simultaneously, yielding high VIFs that reinforce the need for the spread construction step. PHASE 4

Feature Engineering — Spread Construction

The HY-GAGG spread (High Yield minus Global AGG) was the original motivating example: two highly correlated factors where each is significant alone but only their differential is informative in a joint model. This logic is extended systematically to the entire factor universe.

Construction logic

i

Identify correlated pairs: |Corr(A, B)| > 0.70

120 pairs met the correlation threshold from the 28-factor universe.

ii

Both significant in at least one single-factor model

Ensures the pair carries real explanatory power before constructing the spread.

iii

At least one factor loses significance in the full joint model

"Lost significance" (single-factor p ≤ 0.05 → full-model p > 0.05) is the signature of multicollinearity suppressing a real signal. The spread is designed to recover that signal.

iv

Direction: A − B (positive corr) or A + B (negative corr)

For positively correlated pairs, the difference isolates the orthogonal component. All 120 qualifying pairs had positive correlations, so all constructed features are differences. For negatively correlated pairs, addition would combine opposing directional signals — none qualified in this dataset, and the framework handles this automatically.

Key findings from the combined model

  • Asset 2: US Growth − US Value becomes the single most statistically significant feature across the entire analysis (t = 9.9, p = 3.4e-16), suggesting a distinct growth factor tilt beyond plain S&P500 market beta.
  • Asset 5: The US AGG − TIPs spread (t = −11.4) reinforces the TIPs loading (t = 22.5) — when nominal bonds outperform inflation-protected bonds the asset falls, isolating its inflation-protection mandate.
  • Asset 1: US AGG and TIPs survive alongside the US AGG − TIPs spread and US AGG − Global AGG spread, decomposing the bond exposure into domestic nominal, inflation-adjusted, and relative-to-global components.
  • Asset 4: HF FOF Div (t = 6.4) and HF FOF Div − Relative Value HF (t = 6.4) jointly dominate, with secondary High Yield and Global AGG loadings — suggesting a multi-strategy profile with a fixed-income undertone rather than a pure credit fund.
  • Asset 3: Commodities and Event Driven HF survive with multiple Event Driven HF spread features significant, but negative US AGG loading (β = −1.28) persists — consistent with a real assets / commodity-linked allocation that hedges out rate duration.
